Crime prediction models are gaining significant influence with law enforcement resource allocation but could fuel bias against vulnerable populations. We propose the Fairness-Constrained Multi-Task Long Short-Term Memory (FC-MT-LSTM) as the solution for jointly optimizing the accuracy of prediction and fairness among protected groups. The model is compared with 6 baselines i.e. SARIMA, Prophet, Random Forest, XGBoost, CNN-LSTM, Transformer using large scale Indian District level crime data of 21067 records from 36 states on 4 protected groups i.e. Scheduled Castes (SC), Scheduled Tribes (ST), Women and Children. FC-MT-LSTM uses the combination of shared spatio-temporal representation learning and fairness-constrained optimization. Results indicate a balance in performance (Fairness Ratio: 1.99) with good competitive accuracy (MAE: 6.54, R2: 0.9922) whereas performance gaps in fairness are 3.7 to 4.7x as observed for the ensemble methods. The proposed framework makes it possible to forecast crime in a more equitable way.

for future scope:
Several interesting directions can continue the current work. First, the integration of causal inference techniques might allow for the identification of structural drivers of disparities instead of focusing on addressing statistical imbalance. Second, the evolving dynamic constraints of fairness might enable adaptive penalty mechanisms that change as a response to demographic changes and changing social conditions.

Future research may also seek to model intersectionality to address compounded vulnerability experienced by multiple protected attributes (e.g. caste - gender or tribe - age interactions). Incorporating uncertainty quantification, especially in the form of Bayesian deep learning frameworks, could leave along with uncertainty with predictions, helping risk-aware decision saving.

Additionally, cross-domain validation of crime datasets in different countries would contribute to the assessment of generalizability of the proposed framework across different legal and social contexts. Incorporating built-in mechanisms for structured community feedback is also one way of bringing fairness goals back closer to localized understandings of justice and equity. Finally, deployment studies over time are needed to see if progress in algorithmic fairness can be reflected in tangible effects in society.

While the proposed FC-MT-LSTM framework marks progress in the equitable forecasting of crime, there is great scope for the sustained advancement of such models which will require both a constant improvement in the techniques used, as well as broader interdisciplinary collaboration.
