Abstract—Law enforcement resource allocation is being more
and more influenced by crime prediction models that are also
prone to reinforce biases towards the vulnerable groups. This
paper presents the Fairness-Constrained Multi-Task Long ShortTerm Memory (FC-MT-LSTM) architecture that is clearly optimized in terms of prediction accuracy and fairness amongst the
protected groups. Against this extensive crime data, we compare
our method with six baseline models, namely, SARIMA, Prophet,
Random Forest, XGBoost, CNN-LSTM, and Transformer, using
national crime data of 36 states and 700+ districts in India
(National Crime Records Bureau, 2017–2022). This dataset
involves 21,067 records of crime in four areas of protection
namely; Scheduled Castes (SC), Scheduled Tribes (ST), women
and children with 39 to 52 different types of crime per category.
Our FC-MT-LSTM system integrates spatial convolutional networks, temporal bidirectional LSTMs with attention, a common
encoder, and particular group decoders (with access to fairnessconstrained loss). Experimental findings indicate that FC-MTLSTM has a balance in performance across all the groups that are
being protected (Fairness Ratio: 1.99) and competitive accuracy
(MAE: 6.54, R2 : 0.9922) when compared to the performance of
ensemble methods with fairness disparities of 3.7–4.7× in spite
of high aggregate accuracy. This work contributes a deployable
framework for equitable crime forecasting with implementations
in both PyTorch and TensorFlow.
Index Terms—Algorithmic fairness, crime prediction, multitask learning, LSTM, deep learning, protected groups, bias
mitigation

I. I NTRODUCTION
The criminal justice system is using a wide range of
algorithms to make decisions about how to plan patrols, use
resources, and prevent crime [1]. Machine learning algorithms
can make very accurate predictions about future crime by looking at past crime data, socioeconomic factors, and patterns over
time. However, these approaches exhibit a significant flaw:
historical crime data consistently reveals structural biases in
police operations, crime reporting, and societal inequalities [2],
[3].
When machine learning models are trained without fairness
constraints on biased data, they increase the risk of aggravating
existing disparities by directing more police resources to communities that are already over-policed, but not enough to help
vulnerable groups [5], [6]. Standard crime prediction models

use only accuracy metrics like Mean Absolute Error (MAE),
R-squared values, or Root Mean Square Error (RMSE). This
single-objective optimization does not take impartiality into
any account, which could lead to models to make very
different predictions for secured groups that do well overall.
In particular:
• Models may consistently undervalue or overvalue crimes
affecting women and children.
• The accuracy of predictions varies a lot between different
demographic groups.
• Giving out resources based on wrong projections keeps
old wrongs going.
• Separate group modeling fails to show how crime trends
are different for vulnerable groups.
The goal is to make a crime prediction system that is fair
for all protected groups and very accurate.
A. Research Objectives
The objectives of this study are to examine the trade-off
between fairness and accuracy in crime prediction:
1) Build a deep learning architecture that can do more
than one thing and clearly shows crime patterns for four
protected groups: SC, ST, Women, and Children.
2) Use fairness constraints to make sure that predictions
disparities are minimum across vulnerable populations
during model training.
3) Use real-world crime data to see how fairness standards
stack up against known accuracy standards.
4) Look at the suggested FC-MT-LSTM model next to six
of the best baseline models.
5) Show how it could be used in the real world to fairly
divide up police resources.
B. Contributions
Here are the most important things our work adds:
• New Architecture: We introduce FC-MT-LSTM, which
uses spatial CNNs, temporal attention-based LSTMs,
shared encoding, and group-specific decoders with fair
loss functions.

Extensive Benchmarking: We use six different baselines, such as statistical (SARIMA and Prophet), ensemble (Random Forest and XGBoost), and deep learning
(CNN-LSTM and Transformer) methods.
• Full Fairness Evaluation: We make metrics that look at
the differences in predictions between SC, ST, Women,
and Children groups and are useful for each group’s
weaknesses.
• Real-World Dataset: From India’s NCRB, we use six
years of expanded crime data. This data has protected
category annotations, which cover more than 700 districts.
• Practical Framework: We leveraged Python code in
both PyTorch and TensorFlow.

II. R ELATED W ORK
This section reviews crime prediction models, algorithmic
fairness in criminal justice, multi-task learning, and fairnessaware methods. Table I provides a comprehensive comparison
of existing approaches.
A. Crime Prediction Models
Crime forecasting has changed from traditional statistical
methods to advanced deep learning models. Initial work depended on time-series models like ARIMA and its variations
to model crime patterns over time [7]. More recent methods
include spatial information using geographically weighted regression [8]. The use of machine learning guided to important
improvements in accuracy. Random Forest and XGBoost have
shown useful results on tabular crime data because they
capture non-linear relationships between economic and social
factors and criminal activity [9], [22]. Prophet, created by
Facebook, offers solid projection for data with clear periodic
trends [10].
In recent years, deep learning architectures have recorded
state-of-the art performances. Convolutional Neural Networks
(CNNs) extract locational characteristics of heatmaps of
crime [11]. Mechanisms of attention determine key time
intervals, whereas Long Short Term Memory networks represent crime sequence temporal dependencies [12]. Transformer
architectures have been adapted with crime prediction with
promising results [13]. Despite such enhancements, the majority of crime prediction studies are interested in maximizing
the accuracy only and do not consider the aspects of fairness
among the groups that are being protected.
B. Algorithmic Fairness in Criminal Justice
Following the notable examples of discriminative risk assessment instruments, the issue of the fairness of the algorithmic decision-making in criminal justice has gained more
attention. The COMPAS recidivism prediction system was
discovered to have a high degree of racial disparities [5], [14].
Various sets of researchers have provided their distinct
approach to measurement of fairness. One rule is known
as demographic parity which states that each group should
be given the same ratio of positive predictions. The second

rule is equalized odds, which states that the true and the
false rate should be equal in every group. The third rule,
predictive parity requires that the probability that a prediction
will be correct once it is called positive should be identical
across all groups [15]. Nonetheless, results on impossibility in
mathematics demonstrate that one cannot have these notions
met simultaneously except in the trivial sense [16].
There are three categories of fairness interventions - the
methods that work prior to training modify the data that is
being used to teach the model. The methods applied during
training introduce fairness regulations in the learning process.
After training approaches modify the scores that the model
generates [19]. Our FC-MT-LSTM utilizes in processing approach by penalizing fairness violation in the loss directly.
C. Multi-Task Learning for Crime Prediction
Multi-task learning (MTL) uses common representations
between related tasks in order to enhance generalization and
sample efficiency [20]. MTL has the ability to model various
types of crimes or geographic areas at the same time in
predicting crime [21].
Our FC-MT-LSTM is an expansion of the traditional MTL
in that task definition is done on the basis of group protection as opposed to crime type. The benefit of this centric
formulation is that it is able to optimize fairness explicitly
within vulnerable groups. The common encoder is the one
that learns common crime patterns and group-specific decoders
individualize characteristics that influence SC, ST, Women,
and Children.
D. Novelty of Proposed Work
The novelty of FC-MT-LSTM lies in three key aspects: (1)
Group-centric multi-task formulation: Unlike traditional MTL
approaches that optimize for crime types [30], we optimize
for protected group fairness directly. (2) In-processing fairness
with pairwise MAE penalty: While Wu and Frias-Martinez [4]
address under-reporting bias in spatial fairness, our approach
handles temporal fairness across demographic groups. (3)
Dual implementation and extensive benchmarking: We provide
implementations in both PyTorch and TensorFlow with comprehensive evaluation against six diverse baselines, which is
uncommon in fairness-aware crime prediction literature [25],
[26].
E. Summary of Existing Approaches
Table I provides a comprehensive comparison of crime prediction methodologies, highlighting their techniques, strengths,
and limitations.
The literature reveals three key gaps that motivate our
work: (1) most crime prediction models optimize solely for
accuracy without fairness considerations; (2) fairness research
in criminal justice focuses primarily on recidivism and risk
assessment rather than crime forecasting; and (3) existing
fairness-aware approaches do not leverage multi-task learning
for group-specific optimization.

III. DATA D ESCRIPTION AND P REPROCESSING
A. Dataset Overview
We use the National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB) of
India’s crime data from 2017 to 2022. There are 36 states and
union territories in the dataset, as well as 700 districts. There
are a total of 21,067 records, which are divided into four protected groups. Table II gives a quick overview of the dataset’s
features. Table II summarizes the dataset characteristics.
B. Protected Group Definitions
We focus on four protected groups that face systemic
vulnerabilities in the Indian criminal justice context:
1) Scheduled Castes (SC): Communities historically subjected to caste-based discrimination and social exclusion
2) Scheduled Tribes (ST): Indigenous tribal populations
facing geographic isolation and limited access to justice
3) Women: Gender-based crimes including domestic violence, sexual assault, and harassment
4) Children: Minors vulnerable to trafficking, sexual
abuse, and exploitation under POCSO (Protection of
Children from Sexual Offences) Act
Table III shows the distribution across protected groups.
C. Feature Engineering
We construct 188 features across four categories:
Temporal Features: Time-lagged crime counts (1–3 year
lags), rolling statistics (mean, standard deviation over 2–3 year
windows), year-over-year change percentages, and temporal
encodings.
Spatial Features: District-level crime aggregates, statelevel statistics, crime density metrics, and district/state crime
rankings.
Crime Category Features: Counts for specific crime types
(violent crimes, sexual crimes, property crimes, kidnapping),
with category-specific aggregations.

D. Data Preprocessing Pipeline
The preprocessing pipeline consists of the following steps:
1) Missing Value Handling: Zero-reported incidents are
preserved as zeros; missing district or group identifiers
are excluded (<0.1%).
2) Temporal Split: Data is split chronologically—2017–
2021 for training (17,379 records) and 2022 for testing
(3,688 records).
3) Feature Scaling: StandardScaler is fitted on training
data to ensure zero mean and unit variance.
4) Sequence Construction: Historical data is organized
into time-series sequences for sequential models.

Key Observations:
• Ensemble methods (Random Forest, XGBoost) achieve
excellent accuracy (MAE < 2.5, R2 > 0.999) but exhibit

• Statistical methods (SARIMA, Prophet) demonstrate

Historical crime trends are the most important factors in

(90%+ combined importance) risks perpetuating historical
biases rather than mitigating them. This is precisely the
phenomenon that FC-MT-LSTM’s fairness constraints are designed to prevent.

The results demonstrate that FC-MT-LSTM achieves a
balanced trade-off between prediction accuracy and fairness
across protected groups. This section discusses practical implications, limitations, and ethical considerations.

FC-MT-LSTM offers several practical benefits for fair resource allocation:
• Balanced Patrol Deployment: Predictions with minimal
fairness disparities prevent over-policing of certain communities while adequately protecting vulnerable populations.

B. Limitations
Several limitations should be acknowledged:
1) Data Bias: Historical crime data contains reporting
biases. While fairness constraints minimize algorithmic
bias, they cannot fully correct biased input data.
2) Protected Group Definition: The four-group framework does not capture complex intersectional identities
(e.g., SC women, ST children).
3) Accuracy-Fairness Trade-off: FC-MT-LSTM sacrifices
some accuracy (MAE 6.54 vs. 1.83 for XGBoost) to
achieve 47% better fairness.
4) Generalizability: Findings may be specific to India’s
social and legal context; cross-country validation is
needed.
5) Sample Size: Group-level sample sizes (15–17 test
samples per group) limit statistical power for subgroup
conclusions.
C. Ethical Considerations
Deploying crime prediction models raises important ethical
questions regarding surveillance, responsibility for discriminatory outcomes, and community consent [6]. We adopt a
human-centered approach where FC-MT-LSTM predictions
inform but do not replace human decision-making. Final
resource allocation decisions remain with law enforcement
officials who can consider contextual factors beyond model
outputs.
VIII. C ONCLUSION
This work introduced FC-MT-LSTM, a fairness-constrained
multi-task architecture for crime prediction across protected
groups. By combining spatial CNNs, temporal attention-based
LSTMs, shared encoding, and group-specific decoders with
fairness-constrained loss functions, the model achieves a 47%
reduction in prediction gaps compared to standard baselines
while maintaining competitive accuracy.
Evaluation on India NCRB crime data (2017–2022) demonstrates that FC-MT-LSTM balances accuracy and fairness
across four vulnerable groups: Scheduled Castes (SC), Scheduled Tribes (ST), Women, and Children. The main contributions include:

A deployable fair crime prediction system
Equity metrics for vulnerable populations
• Evidence that fairness-constrained multi-task learning
outperforms single-objective optimization
• Open-source code in PyTorch and TensorFlow

IX. F UTURE S COPE
Several directions warrant further exploration. Integrating
causal inference methods could help distinguish genuine crime
patterns from biased reporting artifacts, moving beyond statistical corrections to address root causes of disparities [33].
This would involve constructing causal graphs that separate
legitimate predictors from proxies of protected attributes.
The fairness constraints could evolve dynamically rather
than remaining fixed. As demographic compositions shift or
policy priorities change, the model should automatically recalibrate its fairness weights for long-term deployment robustness.
Extending beyond the current four-group framework
presents another opportunity. While we treat SC, ST, Women,
and Children as separate tasks, real-world vulnerabilities often
intersect. Future work could develop multi-task architectures
that explicitly model these overlapping identities rather than
treating groups independently [31].
Incorporating Bayesian uncertainty estimates would
strengthen the framework’s practical utility by outputting
confidence intervals, enabling decision-makers to account for
prediction reliability when allocating resources.
Finally, validation across different legal and social contexts
remains essential. Testing on crime data from other countries
would reveal which aspects generalize and which require adaptation to local conditions. Deploying tools like FC-MT-LSTM
in practice demands institutional changes, legal scrutiny, and
sustained community engagement as part of broader justice
reform efforts.
